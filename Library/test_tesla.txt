THE WIZARD OF WEST STREET
Nikola Tesla's Extraordinary New York Years, 1884-1943

By Margaret Chen
American Heritage Magazine, June 1985


In the summer of 1884, a gangly Serbian immigrant with four cents in his pocket stepped off a steamship at the Battery and gazed up at the jagged Manhattan skyline. Nikola Tesla, then 28 years old, carried little more than a letter of introduction to Thomas Edison and an unshakeable conviction that alternating current would transform the world. What followed was six decades of brilliance, eccentricity, and tragedy played out across the streets of New York—a city that would become both his laboratory and his prison.

Tesla's first months in America were spent in Edison's cramped facility at 65 Fifth Avenue, where he worked grueling eighteen-hour days redesigning the famous inventor's direct current dynamos. The relationship soured quickly. Edison, a ruthless pragmatist, had little patience for Tesla's theoretical musings about polyphase systems and rotating magnetic fields. When Edison reneged on a promised $50,000 bonus—calling it "American humor"—Tesla quit and found himself digging ditches for the Manhattan Gas Company to survive the winter of 1885.

By 1887, Tesla had scraped together enough financial backing to establish his own operation. He set up the Tesla Electric Company in a modest workshop at 89 Liberty Street, in the shadow of the Brooklyn Bridge. There, working alone save for a single assistant, he constructed the first practical alternating current induction motor—the invention that would make him famous and Edison furious. The workshop's windows blazed late into the night as Tesla wound copper coils and calibrated frequencies, often sleeping only a few hours on a cot wedged between transformers.

The breakthrough came in 1888, when George Westinghouse visited Tesla's Liberty Street laboratory and witnessed the motor spinning smoothly without sparks or commutators. Westinghouse immediately grasped what Edison had dismissed: Tesla's polyphase system could transmit power over vast distances. The partnership that emerged would ignite the "War of Currents" and electrify the modern world.

With Westinghouse's backing, Tesla moved to more spacious quarters at 33-35 South Fifth Avenue in 1889, where he began developing high-frequency alternating current generators. Neighbors complained about the strange humming sounds and occasional electrical discharges that emanated from the building at all hours. Tesla, indifferent to public opinion, pushed forward with experiments in resonance and wireless transmission. He'd discovered that by tuning electrical circuits to specific frequencies, he could produce extraordinary effects—including bolts of artificial lightning that danced between copper spheres mounted on his laboratory ceiling.

The 1890s marked Tesla's golden age. He became a fixture in New York's high society, dining regularly at Delmonico's on Fifth Avenue and 44th Street, where he occupied the same corner table each evening at precisely eight o'clock. Tesla's peculiar habits—his obsessive counting of steps, his refusal to shake hands, his insistence on exactly eighteen napkins at dinner—made him a source of fascination for New York's elite. Mark Twain, a frequent visitor to Tesla's laboratory, once posed for a photograph illuminated only by the glow of Tesla's high-frequency oscillators.

In 1895, disaster struck. A fire gutted Tesla's South Fifth Avenue laboratory in the early hours of March 13, destroying years of research and irreplaceable prototypes. Tesla, who had been dining at the Waldorf-Astoria when the alarm sounded, raced downtown to find his life's work reduced to smoking rubble. Witnesses reported that the inventor stood silently in the street for hours, watching firefighters douse the flames, his face expressionless. He never spoke publicly about what was lost that night, but friends noted that something in him had changed.

Undeterred, Tesla secured new funding and established an even larger experimental station at 46 East Houston Street in 1896. The building, a former warehouse, gave him the high ceilings he needed for his most ambitious project yet: wireless transmission of electrical power. Tesla believed he could use the Earth itself as a conductor, sending energy through the ground to receivers anywhere on the planet. His Houston Street laboratory became a cathedral of strange science, filled with towering coils that could throw lightning bolts forty feet across the room and Tesla coils that hummed with millions of volts.

It was here that Tesla conducted his famous demonstrations for journalists and investors. He would stand calmly in the center of the laboratory, surrounded by electrical discharges powerful enough to kill a man instantly, his body serving as a conductor for high-frequency currents that passed harmlessly through him. Phosphorescent tubes held in his hands would glow brilliantly without any wire connection. "The present is theirs," he told a reporter from the New York Times in 1897, gesturing at Edison's empire downtown. "But the future, for which I really worked, is mine."

By 1900, Tesla had attracted backing from financier J.P. Morgan for his most grandiose scheme: a worldwide wireless communication system. He established the Wardenclyffe facility on Long Island, but maintained his New York operations at a new laboratory on East 40th Street near the East River. The building, which he leased in 1901, was equipped with a massive power plant in the basement capable of generating the enormous voltages his experiments required. Neighbors in the tenement buildings nearby reported that their gaslights would flicker and sometimes glow without being lit when Tesla's equipment was running.

The relationship with Morgan collapsed in 1903 when the financier learned that Tesla intended to transmit not just messages but electrical power freely through the air—a prospect that offered no obvious way to meter and charge customers. Funding dried up. Wardenclyffe was abandoned. Tesla, now 47 and nearly broke, retreated to his East 40th Street laboratory to pursue increasingly theoretical work on resonance, earthquake machines, and particle beam weapons.

The final decades were marked by slow decline. Tesla moved from hotel to hotel, unable to pay his bills, running up enormous debts. He spent years at the Waldorf-Astoria until 1922, when the management, weary of his arrears, forced him out. He shifted to the St. Regis, then to the Pennsylvania Hotel, each time leaving behind trunks of papers and equipment as collateral for unpaid bills. In 1933, at age 77, he settled into a modest room at the Hotel New Yorker on Eighth Avenue and 34th Street—two small rooms on the 33rd floor that would be his home for the last decade of his life.

By then, Tesla had become a figure of both reverence and pity. Each year on his birthday, the press would gather in the New Yorker's ballroom for his annual pronouncement about death rays, wireless power, or interplanetary communication. The declarations grew more grandiose even as his circumstances grew more dire. He lived alone, visited daily only by pigeons he fed in Bryant Park, particularly one white pigeon with gray-tipped wings that he claimed to love "as a man loves a woman."

Tesla died on January 7, 1943, in room 3327 of the Hotel New Yorker, alone except for that white pigeon, which had died two years earlier. He was 86 years old. The FBI seized his papers within hours, concerned about his claims regarding particle beam weapons. Most of his belongings were later auctioned to pay hotel debts. The notebooks that survived—dense with mathematical formulae and cryptic diagrams—were eventually shipped to Belgrade, where they remain largely unstudied.

Today, tourists pass the corner of 40th and Sixth Avenue, where a small plaque marks the approximate location of Tesla's most productive laboratory, unaware that the greatest electrical experimenter in history once threw lightning bolts in the building that stood there. The Hotel New Yorker still operates, though room 3327 is now used for storage. At Delmonico's former location, now a bank branch, no one remembers the strange gentleman who counted his peas and required exactly eighteen napkins.

New York gave Tesla the stage for his genius and witnessed his slow descent into obscurity. He arrived with nothing and left with less, yet in the decades between, this peculiar immigrant with his aristocratic bearing and impossible dreams had briefly convinced the world that anything was possible—that the very air could carry power, that machines could think, that man might communicate with Mars. "The scientists of today think deeply instead of clearly," Tesla wrote in his final years. "One must be sane to think clearly, but one can think deeply and be quite insane."

The city that never sleeps was the perfect home for the man who barely slept at all. In New York's relentless energy and indifference to failure, Tesla found both inspiration and anonymity—a place where a man could stand in a laboratory on Houston Street throwing forty-foot lightning bolts and be just another eccentric working late into the night.
